Visualizing The Impact

Exploring the connection between Campus Greenery & Student Mindset.

Campus Environmental Footprint

From Satellite to Street: How Campus Landscapes Shape Student Experience

Multi-source geospatial indicators reveal clear environmental contrasts across campuses. Vegetation indices (NDVI, GNDVI, NDWI) consistently separate greener institutions from more built-up ones, while urban signatures (NDBI, night lights, density, built area and height) cluster with higher pollution exposure such as NO₂ and PM2.5. Land-use ratios show most campuses remain crop or tree dominant, yet a subset exhibits heavier concrete footprints and stronger urban intensity. Importantly, perceived greenness aligns closely with satellite-derived vegetation metrics, confirming that what students visually experience matches measurable ecological quality—linking greener landscapes with healthier, less urbanized environments.

"Higher satellite greenness matches both cleaner air and greener perception."
Sentinel Environmental Indices by College

Colleges show strong variation in vegetation, built-up, and water indices, clearly separating greener campuses (higher NDVI/NDWI) from more urban/built campuses (higher NDBI/NDBSI).

Land Use/Land Cover (LULC) Ratios by College

Most campuses are crop/green-dominant with smaller built-up fractions, while a few institutions stand out with higher urban or tree cover mixes, highlighting diverse campus landscapes.

Visual Greenness vs NDVI/GNDVI/NBR

Perceived visual greenness rises consistently with NDVI, GNDVI, and NBR, showing that higher user-rated greenery closely matches objectively greener satellite indices.

Socio-Urban & Pollution Metrics by College

Urbanization indicators (night lights, density, built area/height) and pollutants (NO₂, PM2.5) cluster together, showing that more urbanized campuses tend to have higher activity and exposure levels compared to less dense ones.

Mood Analysis

Green Campuses, Happier Minds

Mood trends across colleges consistently reflect the influence of campus environment and daily exposure patterns rather than demographics alone. While gender differences remain minimal, factors such as time spent outdoors, access to greenery, and the type of campus spaces students use play a much stronger role in shaping emotional well-being. Campuses with greener landscapes and active open areas show noticeably higher and more stable mood distributions, whereas pollution and academic or financial pressures correspond with dips in overall happiness.

"Across colleges, average mood shifts upward from moderate (~6–7) to clearly positive (~8–9) when students spend more time in green/open areas, engage with sports grounds, or prefer open-air settings; satellite greenness (NDVI/GNDVI) aligns with these gains, while higher PM2.5, heavy workload, and financial stress show the most consistent downward pull—highlighting that everyday environmental exposure acts as a quiet but powerful buffer for student well-being."

Focus Improvement by Mood Level

Focus improvement rises sharply with mood, peaking at ~85–87% for above-average and high mood groups, showing better mood strongly enhances concentration.

Mood by Urban Classification

Urban and rural students report higher, steadier moods, whereas suburban students show the widest variability and lower medians.

Mood vs Time Spent in Green Areas

More time in green/open spaces strongly correlates with higher mood—ratings steadily climb as exposure increases.

Mood by Contributing Factors

Social interaction, good weather, and physical health lift mood most, while heavy workload and financial stress drag it down.

Mood by Campus Area Exposure

Using sports grounds and green/open areas links to higher moods, whereas library/academic block use shows neutral or slightly lower mood.

Mood by Open-Air Class Preference

Students who favor open-air classes (frequently/occasionally) report higher moods than those who rarely or never prefer them.

Mood vs Environmental Parameters

Greener surroundings (higher NDVI/GNDVI) slightly boost mood, pollution (PM2.5) lowers it most clearly, while temperature (LST) and NO₂ show weak effects.

Stress Analysis

Greener Spaces, Lighter Minds

Stress patterns across colleges reveal strong links to environment, lifestyle, and campus design. While gender differences remain small, hostel students and highly urban settings show slightly higher and more variable stress levels. Time spent in green/open spaces consistently reduces stress, with distributions tightening and medians dropping as outdoor exposure increases. Social interaction, good weather, and physical health act as buffers, whereas workload, academics, and financial pressures elevate stress. Campus spaces also matter—sports grounds and green areas correspond with lower stress, while academic blocks and indoor-heavy zones trend higher. Environmental indicators reinforce this pattern, showing stress rising with built-up intensity and falling with cleaner, greener surroundings.

"More greenery and outdoor time consistently lower stress, while workload and urban intensity push it higher."
Average Stress by College

Stress varies sharply across colleges (≈2-9), with a few campuses experiencing consistently high stress while others remain notably low.

Stress by Gender & Residence Type

Male and hostel/on-campus students show marginally higher and more dispersed stress compared to female and day scholars.

Stress Distribution by Open-Air Class Preference

Students who frequently/occasionally prefer open-air classes report slightly lower stress, while rarely/never groups show higher or more variable stress.

Stress vs Time Spent in Green Areas

More hours spent in green spaces generally associate with lower median stress, suggesting nature exposure buffers stress.

Stress by Contributing Factors

Workload, academics/career pressure, and financial strain elevate stress most, while positive environment, weather, and health correspond to lower stress.

Stress by Campus Area Exposure

Using sports grounds and green/open areas links to reduced stress, whereas heavy academic block and cafeteria use aligns with slightly higher stress.

Holistic Analysis

The Well-being Triad

We mapped the cascading effect of the environment on the student psyche. The data reveals a clear chain reaction: better greenery improves mood, higher mood acts as a buffer against stress, and reduced stress unlocks superior academic focus.

"Green campuses exhibit a distinct 'Well-being Advantage': they cluster at higher mood ratings and lower stress levels, proving that environmental design is a foundational pillar of student mental health."
Mood vs Stress Trend by College

Across colleges, higher mood generally aligns with lower stress, showing a healthy inverse relationship between well-being and pressure.

Mood vs Stress Regression

There is a clear negative relationship—stress decreases as mood increases, indicating better emotional states associate with lower stress.

Impact of Campus Greenery

Better greenery maintenance and higher green–concrete ratios steadily raise mood and reduce stress.

Well-being: Green vs Non-Green

Green campuses show higher average mood and noticeably lower stress than non-green campuses.

Distribution by Campus Type

Green campuses cluster at higher mood ratings and moderate stress, while non-green campuses show more low-mood and higher-stress responses.

Metric Breakdown by College

Most green campuses consistently exhibit higher mood and lower stress per college, whereas non-green campuses display wider variability and more stress-heavy profiles.

Green Areas, Stress & Focus

Exposure to green areas reduces stress and boosts focus across all mood levels, with the strongest benefits seen among average-to-high mood students.
